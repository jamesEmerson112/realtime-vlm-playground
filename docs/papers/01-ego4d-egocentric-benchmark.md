# Ego4D: Around the World in 3,000 Hours of Egocentric Video

## Abstract

We introduce Ego4D, a massive-scale egocentric video dataset and benchmark suite. Ego4D offers 3,670 hours of daily-life activity video spanning hundreds of scenarios (household, outdoor, workplace, leisure, etc.) captured by 931 unique camera wearers from 74 worldwide locations and 9 different countries. The approach to collection is designed to uphold rigorous privacy and ethics standards with consenting participants and robust de-identification procedures where relevant. Ego4D dramatically expands the volume of diverse egocentric video footage publicly available to the research community. Portions of the video are accompanied by audio, 3D meshes of the environment, eye gaze, stereo, and/or synchronized videos from multiple egocentric cameras at the same event. Furthermore, we present a host of new benchmark challenges centered around understanding the first-person visual experience in the past (querying an episodic memory), present (analyzing hand-object manipulation, audio-visual conversation, and social interactions), and future (forecasting activities). More than 80 researchers from 14 institutions worldwide contributed to the dataset and benchmark suite, which is the culmination of an intensive three-year effort.

## Introduction

The input to our proposed benchmarks is a long, fluid video stream from the first-person or "egocentric" point of view -- where we see the world through the eyes of an agent actively engaged with its environment. Whereas Internet photos are intentionally captured by a human photographer, images from an always-on wearable egocentric camera lack this active curation. Wearable cameras capture the visual experience of a person's daily life: what they see, who they interact with, and how they manipulate objects.

The potential of egocentric video is vast. For episodic memory -- specific first-person experiences ("what did I eat?" or "where did I leave my keys?"), to be distinguished from semantic memory ("what is the capital of France?") -- egocentric video provides a natural retrieval index. For understanding hand-object manipulation, the egocentric viewpoint reveals fine-grained interactions that are often occluded in third-person video. For social interactions, it captures who the camera wearer engages with, including conversational dynamics and gaze patterns. For activity forecasting, the first-person perspective provides a unique window into the camera wearer's intentions and goals.

Despite this promise, progress in egocentric perception has been impeded by the lack of large-scale, diverse video data. Existing egocentric datasets are either small, domain-specific, or limited in their annotation types. Ego4D is designed to address these limitations by providing an order-of-magnitude increase in data scale, diversity of scenarios, geographic coverage, and richness of multimodal signals. The dataset is accompanied by five benchmark tasks spanning the essential components of egocentric perception: (1) Episodic Memory -- querying past visual experiences, (2) Hand and Object Interactions -- understanding fine-grained manipulation, (3) Audio-Visual Diarization -- analyzing who said what and when, (4) Social Interactions -- understanding interpersonal dynamics from a first-person perspective, and (5) Forecasting -- anticipating future activities and trajectories.

## Conclusion

We introduced Ego4D, a massive-scale egocentric video dataset and benchmark suite. The dataset dramatically expands the volume of diverse egocentric video footage publicly available to the research community, offering 3,670 hours of daily-life activity video spanning hundreds of scenarios captured by 931 unique camera wearers from 74 worldwide locations across 9 countries. The five benchmark challenges we present are centered around understanding the first-person visual experience in the past (episodic memory), present (hand-object manipulation, audio-visual conversation, social interactions), and future (forecasting activities).

The results on our benchmark suite reveal that egocentric perception presents significant challenges that are not well addressed by models trained on third-person video data. By publicly sharing this massive annotated dataset and benchmark suite, we aim to push the frontier of first-person perception. The research represents the culmination of an intensive three-year effort by a consortium of 14 institutions around the world who came together for the common goal of spurring new research in egocentric perception. We hope Ego4D catalyzes a step change in research on first-person visual understanding, enabling breakthroughs in AR/VR, robotics, and assistive technologies.
